The work

Four pillars. One mission.

FREEDOM was organized as a think-tank collaborative of senior pastors, grassroots leaders, and business and industry — working where faith and economics meet.

01

Economic Development

Financial empowerment with the institutions that move money — from foreclosure prevention with the FDIC and Federal Home Loan Banks to community lending partnerships.

See how this works

Foreclosure prevention and intervention programs built with the FDIC, HUD, and the Federal Home Loan Banks of Atlanta, Boston, and San Francisco — plus state and municipal housing finance authorities — bringing bank-grade tools to church-anchored communities.

02

Education & Training

Workforce and business training designed and delivered nationally — including training for NeighborWorks America, the country's largest HUD housing intermediary.

See how this works

The Connecticut Faith Jobs Collaborative designs workforce programs — race and gender neutral by design — that move residents into building-trade employment and training, through the congregations they already trust.

03

Housing

Homes saved and homes built: foreclosure intervention, housing counseling, and development with state and municipal housing finance authorities.

See how this works

The FREEDOM–Faith CDC collaborative develops faith-based housing — congregations turning land and trust into homes, with the finance authorities at the table from day one.

04

Sustainability

Building congregations and community organizations that outlast any single grant — capacity, governance, and revenue that renews itself.

See how this works

Proof of concept at home: under Rev. McCluster, Shiloh Baptist became the first African-American church in Connecticut to reach energy self-sufficiency through solar.

About

Led from the pulpit and the boardroom.

“Initiate and nurture renewal and restoration for our communities — in faith, with results.”

Rev. Carl McCluster

Senior Pastor of Shiloh Baptist Church in Bridgeport, Connecticut — a pulpit he has held for more than two decades — and Managing Director of FREEDOM. Founder of the Faith Community Development Corporation, the faith-based CDC chartered by Shiloh, and former chairperson of Bridgeport's South End Neighborhood Revitalization Zone.

The fuller story

Trained in both economies — a Bachelor of Arts in Political Economy from Colorado College and a Master of Divinity from the Samuel DeWitt Proctor School of Theology at Virginia Union University. Advisory Board Chair for Faith Acts; Lecturer on Financial Affairs for the Connecticut State Missionary Baptist Convention; former Economic Development Coordinator for the New England Missionary Baptist Convention across nine states; former board member of the Greater Bridgeport Chamber of Commerce, Neighborhood Housing Services of Bridgeport, and the United Way of Greater New Haven. Under his leadership, Shiloh became the first African-American church in Connecticut to become energy self-sufficient through solar.

Remembering Deacon William S. Callion

A tribute to one of FREEDOM's founders — the organization carries his name forward in every city it serves.

The record

Consulting for the FDIC, HUD, and the Federal Home Loan Banks of Atlanta, Boston, and San Francisco. Trainer for NeighborWorks America in eleven cities. More than 100,000 families served through Homes Saved By Faith. Partnerships with governors, U.S. senators, members of Congress, and municipal leaders — plus state housing finance authorities and financial institutions across the country.

The roots

Born in the 1980s as a faith-based answer to high unemployment, a decaying industrial base, and rising crime in Oakland, Atlanta, Connecticut, and the Industrial Midwest — FREEDOM was organized as a think-tank collaborative of senior pastors, grassroots leaders, and business and industry across Texas, Georgia, Maryland and D.C., California, Colorado, Connecticut, Louisiana, and New York, taking each community's best practices and deploying them wherever opportunity presented itself.

Core values

Functional faith · Respect for individuals and organizations · Honesty and integrity · Stewardship · Valuing relationships · Efficient use of resources · Optimizing opportunities.

MLK Corridors Initiative

The street that carries his name should carry the neighborhood.

At least 955 American streets across 41 states bear Dr. King's name. Too many are symbols without investment. The Corridors Initiative changes that — city by city, congregation by congregation.

Bring it to your city

The record in Connecticut

What happened, and who made it happen.

What happened

Three corridors, named and launched.

The initiative launched January 18, 2019 at Spear Park — the corner of MLK and Main Street in Middletown — naming corridor communities in Norwalk, Middletown, and New Britain as epicenters for renewed economic activity, in collaboration with the office of the Governor and the CT Department of Banking.

Who was involved

Pulpits, city halls, and the state.

In New Britain, co-chairs Mayor Erin Stewart and Bishop Dale Shaw jointly lead the collaborative between the City, the Black Ministers Alliance of New Britain, and Freedom, Inc. Statewide: the Connecticut Faith Leaders Collaborative, Freedom, Inc. under Rev. Carl McCluster, the office of the Governor, the CT Department of Banking, and a 2019 program outline to the Department of Economic and Community Development.

Why

Symbols don't hire. Corridors do.

The initiative answers adverse conditions in the neighborhoods around Dr. King's streets through three fronts: economic development & jobs (two of three net new U.S. jobs come from the small businesses these corridors lack capital for), education & training that matches skills to employers' needs, and housing — Connecticut carries the nation's fifth-highest minority homeownership gap, and corridor housing stock has decayed under above-average foreclosure rates and underinvestment.

Cost & effects

The numbers publish here.

Investment figures and measured outcomes for the Connecticut corridors publish in this card as the client supplies them — dollars sought, dollars landed, storefronts opened, jobs created. The framework is built; the numbers drop in.

The pipeline

From "we want this" to funded — five stages.

1 · Join

Tell us about your organization and city — the form asks for everything the pipeline needs, wherever you live.

2 · Research

We confirm your corridor. Does your city have a street named for Dr. King? What condition is it in? Our research automation builds the dossier — including the right officials to contact.

3 · Outreach

City hall hears from us together. Drafted letters to the mayor's office, planning department, and economic development office — reviewed by a human, sent with your name beside ours.

4 · Proposal

The funding ask. A financial-support proposal modeled on the Connecticut playbook — the same kind of program outline that went to DECD — tailored to your city and funders.

5 · Corridor

Your city joins the map. Norwalk, Middletown, and New Britain were first. Your corridor is next.
